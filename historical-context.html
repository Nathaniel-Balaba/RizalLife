Rizal Era

Reform Movement Period

The era when Filipino intellectuals like Rizal advocated for reforms within the Spanish colonial system through peaceful means.

Spanish Colonial System

Social Hierarchy

  • Peninsulares: Spanish-born officials (highest class)
  • Insulares: Spanish descendants born in Philippines
  • Mestizos: Mixed Spanish-Filipino heritage
  • Indios: Native Filipinos (lowest class)

Economic System

  • Encomienda: Land grants to Spanish colonizers
  • Hacienda: Large agricultural estates
  • Polo y Servicios: Forced labor system
  • Tributo: Tax paid by native Filipinos

Religious Control

  • Christianization: Conversion to Catholicism
  • Friars: Spanish priests with significant power
  • Education: Controlled by religious orders
  • Land ownership: Church acquired vast territories

Cultural Impact

  • • Spanish language and customs
  • • Catholic religious practices
  • • European architectural styles
  • • Suppression of local traditions

Social Problems in Rizal's Time

Major Social Issues

Racial Discrimination

Filipinos were treated as second-class citizens, denied equal opportunities in government, education, and business. The colonial system favored Spanish-born officials over qualified Filipinos.

Educational Limitations

Higher education was restricted, with most Filipinos having limited access to quality schooling. The curriculum was designed to maintain colonial control rather than develop critical thinking.

Economic Exploitation

Heavy taxation, forced labor, and monopolistic practices impoverished the Filipino population. The colonial economy was designed to benefit Spain at the expense of local development.

Religious Abuses

Some friars abused their power, engaging in corruption and oppressing the local population. They often interfered in civil matters and accumulated wealth through questionable means.

Impact on Filipino Society

Loss of Identity: Many Filipinos began to lose touch with their pre-colonial culture and traditions, adopting Spanish customs and values while their own heritage was suppressed.

Economic Dependency: The colonial economy was designed to benefit Spain, making the Philippines dependent on the mother country for manufactured goods while serving as a source of raw materials.

Social Stratification: A rigid class system emerged, limiting social mobility and creating deep inequalities that persisted long after the colonial period.

The Reform Movement (Propaganda Movement)

The Reform Movement, also known as the Propaganda Movement, was a peaceful campaign for political reforms in the Philippines during the late 19th century. Led by Filipino intellectuals studying in Europe, including José Rizal, this movement sought to address colonial abuses through legal and diplomatic means.

Key Goals

  • Equal rights for Filipinos
  • Representation in Spanish Cortes
  • Secularization of parishes
  • Educational reforms
  • Freedom of speech and press

Methods

  • Writing and publications
  • Peaceful advocacy
  • International exposure
  • Diplomatic appeals
  • Educational campaigns

Key Figures

  • José Rizal
  • Marcelo H. del Pilar
  • Graciano López Jaena
  • Juan Luna
  • Antonio Luna

How This Context Shaped Rizal

Growing up in this colonial environment profoundly influenced José Rizal's worldview and literary works. His experiences with discrimination, witnessing social injustices, and observing the suffering of his fellow Filipinos motivated him to become a reformist and writer.

Rizal's Literary Response

Noli Me Tangere (1887)

Exposed the corruption and abuses in Philippine society, particularly targeting the friars and colonial officials who exploited their positions of power.

El Filibusterismo (1891)

Depicted the consequences of colonial oppression and the growing revolutionary sentiment among Filipinos who had lost hope in peaceful reform.

Legacy of Understanding

By understanding the historical context of Rizal's time, we can better appreciate why his works were so revolutionary and why they continue to resonate with Filipinos today. His call for reforms, education, and peaceful resistance emerged from his deep understanding of colonial injustices and his vision for a better Philippines.
